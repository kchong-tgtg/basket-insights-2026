Methodology & Data Notes

How this guide was built, what the data represents, and where to apply caution.

🎯 The Question This Answers

For NL basket bundles that are sitting in excess (22+ cover days), what price reduction meaningfully increases rate of sale — and what is the point of diminishing returns on net bundle revenue? The goal is to identify the minimum markdown that achieves the stock clearance target (<7 days ideal, <14 days acceptable) in a single price cut.

📦 Data Sources

Orders & items fct_order_item_parcel
Filter: is_basket_order = TRUE, country NL. Used to identify which item IDs are NL basket bundles and to calculate daily sales (rate of sale).
Price history item_manufacturer_price_history
Filter: price_type = 'price_on_tgtg'. Column: value. Date: valid_from_at_utc. Used to detect price changes and classify them as reductions.
Stock levels fct_item_period_parcel
Filter: period_agg = 'day' only (week/month aggregations not used). Column: available_stock_sum. Stock is joined to the nearest daily record on/before the price change date.

🔢 Key Definitions

Cover days (pre) Stock ÷ pre-discount RoS. Items with zero pre-RoS are assigned 9999 (stuck stock) and still included in the analysis.
Pre / Post RoS Average daily units sold in the 3 days before and 3 days after the price change date. This window is intentionally short to capture the immediate demand response.
Days to clear Stock at time of change ÷ post-discount RoS. Assumes the new RoS is sustained until stock runs out (optimistic, but directionally correct).
Revenue All remaining stock × new discounted price. Not a 3-day window — this is total sell-through revenue at the new price.
Population Only price change events where cover days at the time of the change were 22 or more. This ensures we're analysing decisions made under excess stock conditions.

⚙️ Scenario Calculator Logic

Selling items (RoS > 0): The calculator looks up the average historical % RoS improvement for items in the same price band that received a similar discount. It applies that uplift to your current RoS to estimate post-discount RoS.

Cold-start items (RoS = 0): A percentage multiplier can't be applied to zero. Instead, the calculator uses the average absolute post-discount RoS observed for similar items in that price band — i.e. "items like this, at this discount, typically started selling at X/day."

Days to clear = current stock ÷ estimated post-discount RoS. Revenue = stock × new price.

The discount midpoint is used for new price calculation (5% for <10%, 15% for 10–20%, 25% for 20–30%, 35% for 30–40%, 50% for 40%+).

📊 How Much Data Is Behind This?

~189 total price change events
This is the total number of NL basket price reductions in the data warehouse where cover days were 22 or more at the time of the change. The analysis is a full sweep of all available history — not a sample.

Spread across 7 price bands and 5 discount bands, that works out to an average of roughly 7–8 events per cell. In practice this is uneven: some cells (e.g. €2–€4 at 40%+ off, or €10–€15 at 10–20% off) have 15–18 events, while many others have 3 or fewer.

What "May 2026 snapshot" means: The queries that built this guide were run in May 2026. They retrieve all available history in the data warehouse — there is no manual date cutoff applied. The start date of available NL basket pricing records is not explicitly known; to find out, you could run SELECT MIN(valid_from_at_utc) FROM item_manufacturer_price_history WHERE price_type = 'price_on_tgtg' joined to NL basket items. The end date is effectively May 2026 when the query ran.
Category breakdown
The same 189 events are split by parcel_type_name when the category filter is used. Most categories have very few events per cell — typically 1–4. The two exceptions are:

SNACKS — broadest coverage, multiple price bands with n≥3 per cell.
DRINKS — good coverage in the €2–€8 range.

All other categories (BREAKFAST, HEALTH, KIDS, MEALS, PANTRY, PET FOOD, SEASONAL) should be treated as highly directional. Any cell with n≤3 is flagged in the calculator and matrices.

ℹ️ The dataset will grow over time as Smriti makes more pricing decisions. Re-running the query in 3–6 months will produce more reliable cell estimates, particularly for higher price bands where data is currently thin.

⚠️ Interpretation Caveats

Small n = directional, not definitive. Cells based on 3–8 events should be read as "the data leans this way," not as reliable probability estimates. The outcome bars are more useful than the average RoS number — the distribution shows the range of what actually happened.

RoS averages can be skewed by outliers. A single item going from 0.2/day to 80/day post-markdown pulls the cell average dramatically. In the calculator, also read the confidence bar — if 50% of similar events still ended in excess, a high average RoS doesn't mean the markdown will work for your SKU.
3-day RoS windows are noisy. A single busy weekend or quiet weekday in either the pre or post window shifts the number. The post-RoS is also unlikely to be sustained indefinitely — it captures the initial demand spike.

Days-to-clear assumes constant RoS. The estimate assumes the post-markdown RoS holds steady until stock runs out. In practice, demand may fade after the initial response. Treat the days-to-clear estimate as optimistic — it's a directional target, not a guarantee.
