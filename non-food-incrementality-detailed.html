📐 How to read "AOV lift" for individual SKUs
AOV lift per SKU = average total basket value for orders containing that item, minus the food-only baseline of €39.19.

High-ticket example — Robijn Wasmiddel 3L (€35.99, +€30.37 lift): The 64 orders containing Robijn had an average basket of €69.56. Robijn itself (€35.99) accounts for almost all of that lift. The gap (€35.99 − €30.37 = €5.62) is food displacement — users buying Robijn trimmed their food spend by ~€5.62. The item price directly explains the AOV jump.

Cheap trigger example — Marcel's Green Soap Deo (€3.99, +€10.10 lift): The 230 orders containing Marcel's had an average basket of €49.29. The deo itself only accounts for €3.99 — so why is the basket €10 bigger? Because users who add a cheap, low-effort item are already in full basket-building mode: they're also adding more food and other items. The cheap non-food item is a signal of intent, not the cause of the full lift.
🎯 Commercial action: two different levers
Anchor items (Robijn, Finish, Dove) → negotiate vendor depth, ensure constant availability, promote to mid-AOV users who are €5–10 below FDT threshold. Trigger items (Marcel's, Glorix, Vaseline) → maximise breadth of low-price SKUs, surface early in the browsing flow, use in promotions to drive first non-food conversion. Both levers serve AOV growth, just differently.
How We Analysed This — Full Methodology
The Core Question & Why It's Hard to Answer Simply

The business question sounds simple: "Did non-food increase AOV?" But answering it correctly requires ruling out two alternative explanations:

  1. Selection bias — Maybe people who buy non-food were already high-AOV users before April. If that's true, non-food didn't cause the lift — it just attracted users who were always going to spend more.
  2. Cannibalization — Maybe users added non-food items but removed food items to compensate, keeping total spend flat. If that's true, non-food didn't add revenue — it just reshuffled the basket.

Our analysis is designed to test both of these alternative explanations head-on before claiming a causal effect.

Step 1 — Define the Cohorts

Non-food launched on April 1, 2026 in NL. We split all NL basket users into two groups based on their post-launch behaviour:

  • Non-Food Buyer cohort (n=2,365): Any user with at least one basket order containing a non-food item (parcel_type IN 10, 11, 12) after April 1. Of these, 1,567 (66%) also ordered food-only before April — giving us a before/after view. The other 798 (34%) had no pre-launch basket history, flagged as net-new users.
  • Control group (n=8,064): NL basket buyers active in the post-launch window (Apr–May 2026) who never purchased a non-food item. They're the comparison point for what "normal" post-launch AOV looks like without non-food.

Why this matters: We're not comparing random samples. We're comparing a cohort that self-selected into non-food against those who didn't — which is exactly why the selection bias test in Step 2 is critical.

Step 2 — The Selection Bias Test

To check whether non-food buyers were already high-AOV users, we looked at what they spent before non-food existed.

If non-food buyers' pre-launch median AOV ≈ the control group's post-launch median AOV, they started from the same baseline. Any AOV gap post-launch is therefore caused by the non-food category, not by who the users already were.

Result:

  • Non-food buyers' pre-launch median AOV: €41.05
  • Control group's post-launch median AOV: €40.94
  • Difference: €0.11 — effectively zero

Selection bias is ruled out. These users started identical. The post-launch gap (non-food buyers rose to €43.45) is genuine.

Step 3 — The Revenue Equation: What's Actually Net Incremental?

The key question here is: are we getting truly new revenue, or did non-food spend just replace food spend in the basket?

We broke each non-food buyer's orders into food item spend vs non-food item spend, then compared pre-launch food spend against post-launch food spend:

Pre-launch (food only): €38.72 food items + delivery = ~€40.09 total
Post-launch (food + non-food): €35.52 food items + €9.47 non-food items + delivery = ~€46.36 total
Food displacement: −€3.20 (users spent slightly less on food when adding non-food)
Non-food added: +€9.47
Net incremental revenue per order: +€6.27 (+15.6% on the pre-launch baseline)

The displacement (−€3.20) is real — users do trim food spend slightly when adding non-food. But it's only a 34% offset against the non-food gain. For every €1 of non-food added, €0.66 is net new revenue. Non-food is not replacing food spend — it's growing the basket.

Step 4 — The Cannibalization Check

The final check: are users buying only non-food items and abandoning food entirely?

  • 97.3% of non-food orders also contain food items (1,718 of 1,766 orders are mixed baskets)
  • Only 48 orders (2.7%) are pure non-food — these are edge cases, not a pattern

Non-food is not a substitute category. Users are building bigger baskets on top of their existing food purchases. The -€3.20 food displacement is a mild "ceiling effect" — users near their basket value comfort zone trim food slightly to accommodate non-food — not a structural shift away from food.

Data & technical notes:

Table: tgtg.product_mart.fct_order_item_parcel | country_iso_code = 'NL' | is_basket_order = TRUE | COALESCE(is_cancellation, FALSE) = FALSE AND COALESCE(is_refund, FALSE) = FALSE

Non-food detection: parcel_type IN (10, 11, 12). All three show parcel_type_name = 'UNKNOWN' in the table — a known dbt model gap. Confirmed by sampling: types 10=Personal Care, 11=Skincare, 12=Household (Robijn, Andrélon, Vaseline, Marcel's, Glorix, Finish, Dove).

AOV metric: total_price_incl_tax — basket grand total including delivery. Repeated on every row for the same order_id. Must aggregate at order level first, then average per user.

is_cancellation null gotcha: Normal orders have is_cancellation = NULL (not FALSE). A simple WHERE is_cancellation = FALSE returns zero rows. Correct filter: COALESCE(is_cancellation, FALSE) = FALSE. Same applies to is_refund.

Note on order frequency: Order frequency increased post-April for all NL basket users — both NFB and control groups converged to 0.159 orders/week. This is a platform-wide trend, not a non-food-specific effect. Frequency is not used as a supporting metric for incrementality in this analysis.
