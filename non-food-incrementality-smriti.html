NL Baskets · Did personal care & household genuinely increase AOV, or were these already high-AOV users?

✅
Non-food is genuinely incremental — not selection bias, not cannibalization
Non-food buyers were NOT pre-selected high-AOV users (their pre-launch median €41.05 = control €40.94). Post-launch their median AOV rose to €43.45 (+€2.40 median, +€6.27 mean). 97.3% of non-food orders contain food — additive baskets, not substitutes. Non-food adds €9.47, displaces only €3.20 of food spend: +€6.27 net incremental per order. Non-food is a real AOV driver.

Business Implications
✅ Continue investing in non-food assortment
The AOV lift is real and not explained by cherry-picking power users. Non-food buyers were average users pre-launch who upgraded their baskets post-launch. Growing personal care and household assortment will structurally lift AOV.
✅ Focus on mixed-basket optimisation, not pure non-food orders
97.3% of non-food orders contain food. The value of non-food is as a basket-filler / AOV-lifter, not as a standalone category. Placement, discovery, and bundling with food categories is the strategic lever.
⚠️ Monitor food displacement as non-food scales
Food item spend per order dropped €3.20 (−8.3%). At current scale, the €9.47 non-food contribution more than compensates. If non-food assortment grows and food displacement increases proportionally, net incrementality could compress. Worth tracking quarterly as non-food SKU count expands.

Selection Bias Test — Were Non-Food Buyers Already High-AOV?
The critical test: if non-food buyers were always power-users, their pre-launch AOV would be visibly above the control group. Instead they were essentially identical (€41.05 vs €40.94), confirming the post-launch uplift is caused by non-food, not user self-selection.

Food item spend dropped €3.20 per order (−8.3%) when non-food was added. Non-food contributed +€9.47. Net result: +€6.27 per order is genuinely new revenue.
🧺 Interpretation: additive, not substitutive
97.3% mixed baskets means users are building bigger baskets, not swapping food for non-food. The -€3.20 food spend drop is real but minor — likely a slight ceiling effect where users allocate some food budget to non-food. Net basket value still goes up by €6.27 per order.
⚠️ Monitor food spend per order as non-food grows
Food item spend dropped €3.20 (−8.3%) per order. At current scale this is more than offset by the non-food spend added. But if non-food assortment scales significantly and food displacement increases proportionally, net incrementality could compress. Worth tracking quarterly.
